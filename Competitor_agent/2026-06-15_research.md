## Key Takeaways for Alveos

1. **Oura Ring 5 at €399 sharpens the differentiation imperative.** Oura now matches Alveos's price point with a dramatically smaller form factor and a "proactive health" narrative strikingly similar to Alveos's positioning. The response: double down on what rings structurally cannot do — continuous mouth-vs-nose respiratory detection, mechano-acoustic sensor fusion, and RRV as a novel biomarker. These are not features Oura can add to a ring; they require physical placement at the throat/chest. Make this a visible claim in all Seed pitch and marketing materials.

2. **The FDA wellness guidance is a tailwind — use it, but move fast to build credibility.** The new framework supports Alveos's gray-zone positioning and makes CE-marking strategy cleaner, but the same rules are flooding the market with unvetted devices. As consumer and B2B buyers lose trust in "wellness wearable" claims broadly, clinical validation (University of Kent) becomes Alveos's differentiator and moat. Accelerate publication of accuracy data (99.8% RR, 96.6% oral/nasal) in peer-reviewed form before competitors cite similar numbers without evidence.

3. **Whoop's Abbott/Mayo Clinic investors signal a B2B clinical channel worth scoping.** Institutional health systems are buying into wearable platforms, not just hardware. Alveos's respiratory biomarkers — particularly for COPD, asthma, sleep-disordered breathing, and stress/ANS monitoring — have natural homes in pulmonology and respiratory therapy clinics. Worth a conversation with the University of Kent partner about whether a small pilot study positions Alveos for a clinical B2B play alongside the D2C Kickstarter channel.
